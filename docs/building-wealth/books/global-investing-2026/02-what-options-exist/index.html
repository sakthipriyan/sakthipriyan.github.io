Chapter 2

What Options Exist?

Comparing Mutual Funds, Indian ETFs, and direct LRS investing for global exposure.

Three Routes to Global Exposure

There are three ways an Indian investor can get international diversification. Each has a different level of effort, cost, and flexibility.

Route 1: Domestic Mutual Funds

Funds like ICICI Pru Nasdaq 100 Fund of Fund, Motilal Oswal S&P 500 Index Fund, and Mirae Asset NYSE FANG+ ETF FoF invest your rupees into international markets.

Pros:

  • No LRS, no remittance, no foreign brokerage account
  • Rupee-denominated — buy and redeem like any Indian MF
  • SIP-friendly

Cons:

  • Limited to fund house’s universe of underlying funds
  • SEBI overseas investment cap: when the industry aggregate hits the limit, fund houses suspend fresh purchases
  • ICICI Pru Nasdaq 100 has already suspended fresh subscriptions — reaffirming that this cap is a real operational risk, not a theoretical one
  • Expense ratios are layered (FoF pays TER on top of underlying fund’s TER)

Route 2: Indian ETFs with International Exposure

ETFs like Motilal Oswal Nasdaq 100 ETF (N100) and Mirae Asset NYSE FANG+ ETF trade on Indian exchanges (NSE/BSE).

Pros:

  • Exchange-traded, can buy in real time
  • Generally lower TER than FoF route
  • No LRS needed

Cons:

  • Same SEBI overseas investment cap applies — these ETFs also periodically suspend creation
  • When creation is suspended, ETF trades at a significant premium to NAV
  • Liquidity can be thin in some ETFs
  • You’re still limited to what Indian fund houses have launched

Route 3: Direct International Investing via LRS

Open a foreign brokerage account (e.g., IBKR), remit money under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme, and invest directly in global ETFs — including Irish-domiciled UCITS ETFs.

Pros:

  • Access to the full global universe — any ETF listed on international exchanges
  • No SEBI cap constraint
  • At scale, total cost (FX + brokerage) can be lower than the TER drag of FoF funds
  • Better tax treatment when using accumulating Irish ETFs (deferred gains, no annual dividend)

Cons:

  • Requires setup effort: bank FX Retail configuration, brokerage account KYC
  • FX costs on remittance (aim for < 1%)
  • TCS applies above INR 7 lakh per year (refundable, but capital is blocked)
  • More complex tax filing (Schedule FA, Schedule FSI, Form 67)

Which Route Should You Choose?

Factor Domestic MF Indian ETF LRS + IBKR
Setup effort Minimal Minimal Significant
SEBI cap risk High High None
Tax filing complexity Low Low High
Universe of instruments Narrow Narrow Full global
Cost at scale (>₹5L/yr) High (TER) Medium Low
Recommended for beginners ✅ ✅ ❌

For anyone remitting meaningful amounts (upwards of ₹5–10 lakh per year), the LRS route becomes worthwhile. The route through domestic mutual funds is the simplest starting point, but the SEBI cap risk is real — funds can and do shut fresh subscriptions.
