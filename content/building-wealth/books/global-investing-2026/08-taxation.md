## Why Irish UCITS ETFs Are Tax-Efficient for Indian Investors

The tax treatment of your investment depends critically on *what* you hold and *where* it's domiciled.

| | US-domiciled ETFs (QQQ, VOO) | Irish UCITS ETFs (XNAS, ANAU, CSPX) |
|---|---|---|
| US dividend WHT | 25–30% (deducted in ETF/at source) | 15% (Ireland-US treaty) |
| Annual dividend tax in India | Yes — taxed at slab rate | No (accumulating ETFs: no payout) |
| Schedule FSI filing | Every year (dividends) | Only when selling |
| US estate tax exposure | Yes (above $60K) | No |

For Indian investors using accumulating Irish ETFs, the annual tax reporting overhead is minimal and no dividend tax drag applies during the holding period.

## Capital Gains Tax on Selling

Foreign ETFs are classified as **non-equity instruments** for Indian tax purposes (post Finance Act 2023):

- **Short-term capital gains** (holding < 24 months): taxed at your income slab rate
- **Long-term capital gains** (holding ≥ 24 months): 12.5% without indexation

This is the same treatment as debt mutual funds. The longer you hold, the better — both for the 15% ETF-level WHT advantage (vs compounding under 25–30%) and for qualifying for 24-month LTCG.

## Computing Capital Gains in INR

When you sell, gains are computed in INR:

> **Gain = (Sale proceeds in USD × RBI rate on sale date) − (Purchase cost in USD × RBI rate on purchase date)**

Where to find historical RBI reference rates: [RBI Reference Rates](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/ReferenceRateArchive.aspx)

Record these for every purchase and sale:
- Date of transaction
- USD amount
- RBI reference rate on that date
- INR equivalent

Your bank's remittance receipt shows the INR debit, which serves as the INR cost basis for purchases.

## Annual Compliance: Schedule FA

**Every year**, regardless of whether you sold anything, you must disclose foreign assets in your ITR via **Schedule FA**:

- Foreign brokerage account details (broker name, country, account number)
- Holdings: ETF name, ISIN, country of domicile, number of units, acquisition cost in INR, year-end value in INR

Non-disclosure of foreign assets attracts severe penalties under the **Black Money (Undisclosed Foreign Income and Assets) and Imposition of Tax Act, 2015**. This is not optional.

## On-Sale Compliance: Schedule FSI and Form 67

When you **sell** Irish UCITS ETFs:

1. **Schedule FSI** (Foreign Source Income): Report the capital gain with source country details
2. **Form 67**: If claiming Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) for WHT on dividends (if you held distributing ETFs or US ETFs), Form 67 must be filed *before* filing the ITR for that year

For accumulating Irish ETFs: no dividend payout means no FSI to report during holding. Schedule FSI only becomes active in the year of sale.

## US ETFs vs Irish ETFs: Annual Tax Overhead Comparison

| Activity | US ETFs (QQQ, VOO) | Irish UCITS ETFs (XNAS, ANAU) |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule FA | Every year ✅ | Every year ✅ |
| Schedule FSI | Every year (dividends) | Only on sale |
| Form 67 | Every year (30% WHT claim) | Only on sale (if applicable) |
| Dividend tax paid | Slab rate, annually | None during holding |
| WHT drag on compounding | 25–30% every year | 15%, contained inside ETF |

Irish UCITS accumulating ETFs require significantly less annual administration and compound more efficiently.

## ITR Form

Use **ITR-2** (not ITR-1) if you have foreign assets or foreign income. If you also have business income, use ITR-3.
